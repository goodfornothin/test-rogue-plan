Teaching Without a Script: Inside a Rogue Bachata Class

23 July 2026 · Featuring Bo, Rogue Bachata co-founder

Walk into a Rogue Bachata class on a Tuesday at Cafe Sol or a Wednesday in King's Cross, and you won't find a set routine written on a whiteboard. There's no fixed syllabus to work through, no sequence of steps that gets drilled the same way every week. According to Bo, who co-founded and helps run the classes, that's entirely deliberate.

No script, on purpose

Bo is blunt about the underlying philosophy: the whole point of how the classes are taught is that there isn't a set script to follow. "Within dance and within social dancing, the goal is to not have set moves," she says. "Things are constantly evolving and changing — I don't feel like it's ever set." For a partner dance built on improvisation, that's less a stylistic choice than a matter of staying true to what social dancing actually is.

How a class actually runs

In practice, that philosophy shapes the structure of every session. Classes open with the basics, giving beginners time to get comfortable with the fundamentals and try them out to music, before loosening into what Bo describes as a framework rather than a fixed sequence — different holds, a few counts of eight set aside specifically for people to try their own thing. "We tell them: do your own thing," she says.

Where "so-and-so's move" comes from

That same instinct toward personal style shows up in a habit that started almost by accident, back in one of the earliest lessons Oscar taught. When a student in class does something slightly different from what's being demonstrated, instead of correcting it, the instructors will often fold it straight into the lesson — and name it after them. It becomes "so-and-so's move." Bo sees it as more than a running joke. "I hope that encourages people to dance how they want, in their own style," she says.

Making room for beginners

None of this, Bo is careful to note, comes at the expense of newcomers. If anything, the lack of a rigid script is what makes the room feel low-stakes. "It's really friendly and welcoming for beginners," she says. "There's no pressure, and participants don't feel like they're worried about what they looked like."

The salsa dancers who stay for bachata

One recurring surprise, according to Bo, is who walks through the door expecting something else entirely. Plenty of newcomers arrive assuming they're about to learn salsa, and there's often a flicker of disappointment when they realise it's bachata instead. It rarely lasts. "They end up loving it way more than they expected," Bo says, "and they get converted."

Somewhere between the loose framework, the borrowed moves, and the salsa dancers won over despite themselves, a pattern emerges: Rogue Bachata's classes are built to keep changing shape around the people in the room, rather than the other way around. Come see what that looks like on a Tuesday or Wednesday. No partner needed.
