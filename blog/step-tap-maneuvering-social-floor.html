Step Tap, Step Tap: An Exercise for Moving Around the Room

15 July 2026 · The morning after a Tuesday at Cafe Sol

A lot of beginner leaders ask the same question, in different words: what do I actually need to know? After a well-attended Tuesday at Cafe Sol, the answer was refreshingly simple: "If you know a few things, just do them really well according to the music and in time, and your partner will enjoy it." But one gap kept showing up in the room — beginners who could do a basic step just fine, standing in one spot, doing nothing else all night.

The skill nobody names: maneuvering

The exercise that came out of it was deliberately simple: step tap, step tap, changing weight — the kind of pattern any beginner can do without thinking. First in one spot. Then, taking a partner, moving around the room while doing it. That second part is the actual point. "That is quite a skill, to be a dynamic dancer — we don't want to be static."

Standing in one spot dancing perfectly is a different skill from navigating a room full of other couples, all moving, all unpredictable, while staying in time and in connection with your own partner. Most beginners have never practised that second thing at all — because most classes only ever teach the first.

The research

This mirrors a well-established idea in motor learning, going back to the Russian physiologist Nikolai Bernstein. Beginners naturally "freeze" extra degrees of freedom — locking down everything except the one thing they're currently working on — because trying to control too many variables at once overwhelms the nervous system. Stripping the step down to something so simple it requires no thought (step tap, step tap) is exactly what frees up the attention needed to learn the *next* skill — maneuvering — without the steps themselves competing for the same mental bandwidth. Once maneuvering stops requiring conscious effort, dancers gradually "unfreeze" and layer complexity back in — which is the same order the exercise is built in: spot, then space, then choose your own steps once the room stops being the hard part.

Protecting your first experiences on the floor

The advice that followed was more personal. Some followers, especially early on, are chasing something flashy — and the guidance to beginner leaders was direct: "Do not bother about it. If that's the case, just don't dance with them." Leading is already vulnerable enough as a beginner without also absorbing someone else's disappointment that you're not "advanced" yet. Rogue Bachata's own instructors have been candid about their own start too — years of mostly standing and watching, in conditions once called "quite hostile," where two dances in a night felt like a real win.

The research

This lines up closely with research into why people quit recreational sports and dance early on. Studies consistently find that negative interpersonal experiences — poor treatment from partners or instructors, lack of enjoyment, feeling judged — are among the strongest predictors of dropout, often outweighing the actual difficulty of the skill itself. Protecting a beginner's early social experiences isn't softness; it's the single biggest lever over whether they come back a second time. On the flip side, Bandura's research on self-efficacy shows that small, repeated "mastery experiences" compound into real confidence far more reliably than being thrown into difficulty too early — which is exactly why "two good dances in a night" was enough to keep a beginner coming back all those years ago, and exactly why we build the same small wins into every class now.

There's no fixed lesson plan for a reason — what gets taught depends on who's in the room, their energy, what's missing that night. But the through-line stays the same: build one small, ownable skill at a time, and protect the room while people are still finding their feet in it.

Come practise this with us — beginners always welcome, no partner needed, and nobody's watching for a spectacle.
