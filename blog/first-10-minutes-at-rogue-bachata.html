What Actually Happens in Your First 10 Minutes at Rogue Bachata

14 July 2026 · Answering a question from someone about to come for the first time

This one came from an actual question: "What actually happens in my first 10 minutes if I show up alone? I could be nervous." So here's the honest, specific answer — not "don't worry, everyone's friendly," but exactly what happens, minute by minute.

You'll be welcomed by name — or find out your name

On Wednesdays at Keystone Crescent, that's Oscar, Boadicea, or a teacher on the door. On Tuesdays at Cafe Sol, it's Sam, who runs the venue, and his staff. You'll be asked for your ticket — get that out of the way and you're in. People who've come for the class are already getting ready, the music's playing, and you can just settle in. Sometimes people start chatting to each other, sometimes we'll nudge that along ourselves. Either way — nobody's watching the door waiting to judge who walks in alone.

You are never the only new person

The thing almost everyone assumes when they're nervous about showing up alone is that they'll be the only beginner in a room full of experts. It's essentially never true: usually somewhere close to half the room is new, and half have never danced bachata before in their life. That's not an exception — it's a normal Tuesday or Wednesday.

The research

This exact misperception has a name — pluralistic ignorance: the tendency to assume your own private nervousness is unusual, while everyone else looks confident, when in fact most of the room is privately feeling the same way. A study of incoming Princeton students found this specific pattern predicted higher anxiety, weaker social fit, and a lower sense of belonging among newcomers — and that the fix isn't reassurance, it's correction: openly naming how common the anxiety actually is breaks the illusion. Which is exactly why we say it out loud rather than just hoping you'll relax.

Nobody's thrown straight into a routine

We start with a warm-up, all together, before anyone partners up. No pressure on what to do — follow along at the front, or just move at your own pace, joke around, shake it out. Only after that do we get into partners, mostly in a circle, and rotate — so you never need to have brought someone with you.

The research

Building in a low-stakes, no-pressure stage before anything performance-oriented starts is a documented way to reduce what organisational psychologists call "learning anxiety" — the defensiveness people feel when trying something new in front of others. Research on psychological safety in group learning found that people won't take the risk of trying, failing, or looking clumsy in front of others until they feel secure that they won't be judged for it — and a shared, low-stakes warm-up before anything "counts" is exactly the kind of structure that builds that safety before it's needed.

"We find the rhythm in everybody"

The line that matters most from the video: people show up thinking they can't dance, and — as it's put — "we find the dance in everybody, we find the rhythm in everybody." That's not just a nice thing to say to beginners.

The research

Genuine inability to perceive or move to a musical beat — technically called "beat deafness" — is rare. Research estimates put it at around 3% of people, and it's a specific, identifiable condition affecting how the brain couples movement to changing sound — not a vague personality trait some people "have" and others don't. For the other 97%, rhythm isn't a talent you're born with or without; it's there, and it's trainable. "I can't dance" is almost always a confidence problem wearing a rhythm problem's clothes.

Every ability, every age, whatever level you're at — that's genuinely the room. Half of it is figuring things out right alongside you.

Come find out for yourself — beginners always welcome, no partner needed, and you definitely won't be the only new person.
